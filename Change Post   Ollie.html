<p dir="ltr"><span><span>It&rsquo;s easy to forget that the weighty orders sent down from on high by the Federal Reserve&rsquo;s open-market committee were originally hashed out by humans&mdash;humans who grow restless during meetings, who bicker, who get confused about bureaucratic acronyms, who have to get up and use the restroom every once in a while. They&rsquo;re also humans who make jokes.</span></span></p>
2
3
<p dir="ltr"><span><span>How do we know? Because the Fed&rsquo;s transcripts have painstakingly documented every instance of laughter for decades. Unhelpfully, the transcripts make no distinction between nervous laughter, charitable chuckles, and room-filling guffaws. But they do note, for instance, when committee members made references to drinking with Janet Yellen and then collectively engaged in &quot;[laughter]&quot;. &nbsp;</span></span></p>
4
5
<p dir="ltr"><span><span>After the Fed released its 2009 transcripts two weeks ago, my co-author, Ben Southgate, and I undertook the (sometimes groan-inducing) task of parsing every laugh going back to 1990, the earliest year with searchable transcripts. We set out about this project without any hope of finding a meaningful trend in the data, yet we came away with a surprisingly telling one: Starting in 2000, the frequency of laughs per transcript page grew steadily until late 2007, when the effects of the housing crash were starting to be felt&mdash;and the laughter dropped precipitously. The Fed was literally chuckling all the way up to the recession.</span></span></p>

<p dir="ltr"><span><span>We also looked at who among the Fed was the funniest. During his 18-year reign, Alan Greenspan successfully landed about 550 jokes&mdash;the most of anyone we analyzed&mdash;though Ben Bernanke&rsquo;s 289 is impressive given that his span as chairman has been much shorter. Both of them presumably benefited from the committee&rsquo;s perceived obligations to laugh at their leader&rsquo;s wisecracks. <p dir="ltr"><span><span>But Greenspan and Bernanke racked up so many jokes because they were speaking so frequently, so it&rsquo;s worth noting the heroic performances of TK Moore and TK Driggins, who embody the comedic ideal of a 100 percent hit rate: one statement, one &ldquo;[laughter].&rdquo; At the other end of the spectrum is the stoic TK Keehn, who only let his/her playfulness show itself once in 235 statements.</span></span></p>

<p dir="ltr"><span><span>So what was so funny? Most of the time, the committee was joking about themselves: their own formality, their own hypersensitivity to the implications of their press releases, their own uncertainty about the economy. They also made plenty of earnest, slightly nerdy jokes that would probably have fallen flat if delivered in other rooms. Other jokes relied on low-hanging comedic fruit: the stubborn confidence of Southerners, the evil nature of lawyers. I&rsquo;ll spare you the pain of reading scores of these, but here is a sampling of what made the Fed laugh in 2009:</span></span></p>
